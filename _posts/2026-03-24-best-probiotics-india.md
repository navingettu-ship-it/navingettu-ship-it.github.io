Probiotics have become one of the top-selling supplement categories in India — and the marketing has gotten completely out of hand. "100 billion CFU!" "50 strains!" "Clinically proven!" Almost none of these numbers mean what the packaging implies.

Here's what the evidence actually shows, which numbers matter and which don't, and which products on Amazon India are worth your money.

---

## What Probiotics Actually Do (And Don't Do)

The honest picture from current research:

**What's well-supported:**
- Reducing duration and severity of antibiotic-associated diarrhoea
- Helping manage IBS symptoms (particularly bloating and bowel irregularity) in some patients
- Supporting immune function through gut-immune axis modulation
- Restoring gut microbiome balance after illness or antibiotics

**What's overstated:**
- "Fixing" gut health in people who are generally healthy
- Dramatic weight loss effects
- Systemic benefits from generic multi-strain blends without specific evidence

Probiotics are most impactful for people with **specific gut issues** — IBS, post-antibiotic disruption, chronic bloating, or loose stools. For healthy people with no gut symptoms, the benefit is more modest and varies by individual.

---

## CFU Count: Why Bigger Is Not Better

CFU (Colony Forming Units) measures the number of viable bacteria in a dose. Marketing has created a race to print larger numbers — but the research does not support "more CFU = more benefit."

What actually matters:
- **Strain specificity** — different strains do different things. *Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG* has strong evidence for diarrhoea. *Bifidobacterium longum* is linked to stress and anxiety reduction. A generic "14-strain blend" at 100 billion CFU may do less than a targeted single-strain product at 10 billion.
- **Survivability** — bacteria must survive stomach acid to reach your gut. Look for delayed-release or enteric-coated capsules, or strains with demonstrated acid resistance.
- **Storage** — many probiotics require refrigeration. Products that don't specify "no refrigeration needed" are often dead on arrival after sitting in a warehouse.

## Should You Take Prebiotics Too?

Probiotics introduce beneficial bacteria. **Prebiotics** are fibres that feed those bacteria. Many high-quality probiotic supplements now include both (marketed as "synbiotics").

If your diet is already rich in prebiotic foods — onions, garlic, bananas, oats, legumes — you probably don't need to supplement prebiotics separately. If your diet is low in fibre, look for a formula that includes FOS (fructooligosaccharides) or inulin alongside the probiotics.

---

## When to Take Probiotics

- Take **with or just before a meal** — food buffers stomach acid and improves bacterial survival
- **Consistency matters more than timing** — daily use for at least 4 weeks to assess effect
- **During and after antibiotics:** Take probiotics 2 hours away from the antibiotic dose (not simultaneously) and continue for at least 2 weeks after finishing the antibiotic course
