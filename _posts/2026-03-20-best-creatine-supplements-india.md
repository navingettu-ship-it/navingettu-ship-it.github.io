## What Creatine Actually Does

Creatine is stored in your muscles as phosphocreatine and used to regenerate ATP — the immediate energy currency for short, explosive efforts. Supplementing creatine increases your phosphocreatine stores, which translates to:

- **More reps at a given weight** — typically 1-3 extra reps per set
- **Faster recovery between sets**
- **Greater muscle growth over time** — partly from the extra volume, partly from cell volumisation
- **Modest cognitive benefits** — emerging evidence for memory and processing speed

These are not marketing claims. They are replicated, peer-reviewed outcomes. Creatine is one of the few supplements where the evidence is genuinely strong.

---

## What to Look For

**Creatine monohydrate, not fancy variants.** Creatine HCl, Kre-Alkalyn, buffered creatine — none of these have demonstrated superiority to plain monohydrate in head-to-head trials. Monohydrate is cheaper, better studied, and works just as well. Don't pay a premium for marketing.

**Micronised.** Micronised creatine has smaller particle size, dissolves better in water, and is less likely to cause stomach discomfort. Most reputable products in India now use micronised monohydrate.

**Creapure certification** is a premium German purity standard — products with this badge have been independently tested for heavy metals and banned substances. Worth noting if you're a competitive athlete.

**No loading phase needed.** The old "loading phase" (20g/day for 5 days) saturates muscles faster, but results in the same total creatine levels as simply taking 3-5g/day for 3-4 weeks. Loading is optional — daily consistent use is what matters.

## How to Take Creatine

- **Dose:** 3–5g per day. That's it. Higher doses don't produce better results.
- **Timing:** Doesn't matter much. Post-workout has a slight edge in studies, but consistency matters more than timing.
- **With water or juice:** Creatine uptake is slightly enhanced by insulin — taking it with a carbohydrate source (juice, a meal) is marginally better than water alone.
- **Daily, including rest days:** Creatine works through saturation, not acute dosing. Missing days slows progress.
- **Cycling:** Not necessary. Long-term continuous use is safe based on available evidence.

---

## Common Concerns Addressed

**Does creatine cause hair loss?** One study from 2009 found increased DHT (a hormone linked to hair loss) in rugby players taking creatine. It has not been replicated. Current evidence does not support creatine causing hair loss.

**Does creatine damage kidneys?** Not in healthy individuals at recommended doses. The concern stems from confusion — creatine supplementation raises serum creatinine (a kidney marker) but this is a direct result of increased muscle creatine, not kidney dysfunction.

**Is creatine vegan?** Yes — creatine monohydrate is synthetically produced, not derived from animal sources.
