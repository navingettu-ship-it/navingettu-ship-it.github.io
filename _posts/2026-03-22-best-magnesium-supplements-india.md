Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the human body — energy production, muscle contraction, nerve function, blood sugar regulation, and sleep. It is also one of the most common mineral deficiencies in the modern diet, driven by soil depletion, processed food consumption, and the fact that stress actively depletes magnesium from your tissues.

The frustrating reality: most magnesium supplements sold in India use a cheap, poorly-absorbed form that does little except give you loose stools. Here's how to choose the right one.

---

## Magnesium Forms: What Actually Absorbs

Not all magnesium is equal. The form matters enormously for how much actually reaches your cells.

**Magnesium Oxide** — the most common, cheapest form. Only about 4% bioavailable. Primarily used as a laxative. Avoid for nutritional supplementation.

**Magnesium Citrate** — significantly better absorption (~16-30%). Good for constipation relief and general supplementation. Mild laxative effect at higher doses.

**Magnesium Glycinate (Bisglycinate)** — magnesium bound to glycine, an amino acid. Highest bioavailability of common forms, minimal digestive side effects, and glycine itself has calming properties that complement magnesium's relaxation benefits. Best form for sleep, stress, and general long-term supplementation.

**Magnesium L-Threonate** — a newer form that crosses the blood-brain barrier more effectively than other forms. Emerging evidence for cognitive benefits and memory. The most expensive form.

**The short version:** Buy glycinate for sleep and stress. Citrate if you're also addressing constipation. Avoid oxide entirely.

---

## What Magnesium Actually Helps With

**Sleep:** Magnesium regulates GABA receptors and melatonin production. Multiple studies show supplementation improves sleep onset, sleep quality, and early morning awakening — particularly in older adults and those with deficiency.

**Stress and anxiety:** Magnesium modulates the HPA axis (your body's stress response system). Chronic stress depletes magnesium; low magnesium amplifies stress reactivity — a vicious cycle that supplementation can break.

**Muscle cramps and recovery:** Magnesium is essential for muscle relaxation (calcium contracts muscles; magnesium releases them). Deficiency is a common cause of nocturnal leg cramps.

**Blood sugar:** Magnesium is a cofactor for insulin function. Low magnesium is associated with insulin resistance — relevant in a country where type 2 diabetes rates are high.

## How Much Do You Need?

| Group | Recommended daily intake |
|---|---|
| Adult men | 400–420 mg |
| Adult women | 310–320 mg |
| Pregnant women | 350–360 mg |
| Under chronic stress | 400–500 mg |

Most Indians get around 200-250mg from diet. A supplement providing 200-400mg elemental magnesium (not total salt weight) bridges the gap. Check that the label lists elemental magnesium, not just the weight of the magnesium compound.

## When to Take Magnesium

**For sleep:** 30–60 minutes before bed. Magnesium glycinate taken at night is the single most reported use case — users describe falling asleep faster and waking feeling more rested.

**For muscle cramps:** Before bed, or after training.

**For general supplementation:** With a meal to minimise any digestive sensitivity.

Start at a lower dose (100–200mg elemental) and increase gradually — this minimises the risk of the loose stools that even glycinate can occasionally cause at high doses in sensitive individuals.
